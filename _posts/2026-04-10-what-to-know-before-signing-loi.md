A letter of intent, or LOI, is the document where a buyer puts their offer in writing and both sides agree to move forward toward a potential closing. It is not the closing itself, and in most transactions it is not a binding commitment to complete the sale. But it is the moment when a deal becomes real, and the terms established in the LOI will shape everything that follows.

Most sellers naturally focus on the purchase price. That number matters, but it is only one part of the picture. The exclusivity period, the structure of the transaction, the working capital requirements, and the buyer's ability to actually close can have just as much impact on the outcome as the headline valuation.

## What an LOI Actually Commits You To

Most business acquisition LOIs are largely non-binding, except for certain provisions related to the transaction process itself.

The provisions that are commonly binding include:

- **Exclusivity**
- **Confidentiality**
- **No-shop obligations**

Exclusivity means you agree to stop pursuing other buyers for a specified period while the buyer conducts due diligence. Confidentiality requires both sides to keep the transaction and shared information private. No-shop provisions prevent you from actively soliciting competing offers during the exclusivity period.

These provisions may seem secondary to the purchase price, but they often have a greater practical impact. Once you sign an LOI and enter exclusivity, your options become significantly more limited if the deal slows down or circumstances change.

## Understanding the Exclusivity Period

Exclusivity exists for a legitimate reason. Serious buyers spend significant time and money evaluating a business. They may hire accountants, attorneys, lenders, consultants, and industry specialists before deciding whether to proceed. Most buyers will not make that investment unless they know the seller is committed to working exclusively with them for a period of time.

Pay close attention to both the length of the exclusivity period and the circumstances under which it can be extended.

A hard 60-day exclusivity period is very different from a 60-day period that automatically extends whenever the buyer requests additional time. Longer periods are not necessarily unreasonable, particularly when financing, real estate, or complex diligence is involved. What matters is having a clearly defined process and a clear end point.

There are many legitimate reasons a buyer may need additional time. Lenders often conduct their own underwriting independent of the buyer's diligence process, and that review moves on the lender's timeline regardless of how prepared either side is. Businesses with multiple locations, significant customer contracts, real estate holdings, or complex financial records naturally require more review than simpler operations.

What sellers should be cautious about is open-ended extension language that allows exclusivity to continue indefinitely without clearly defined milestones.

## Asset Sale or Stock Sale

One of the first items established in an LOI is whether the transaction will be structured as an asset sale or a stock sale.

In an asset sale, the buyer purchases specific assets of the business such as equipment, contracts, intellectual property, goodwill, and inventory. The buyer typically selects which liabilities they are willing to assume.

In a stock sale, the buyer acquires the legal entity itself, including all of its assets, obligations, contracts, and historical liabilities.

Most lower-middle-market transactions are structured as asset sales. One reason is lender preference. Many lenders are more comfortable financing asset acquisitions because the collateral structure is simpler and easier to evaluate.

From a seller's perspective, the structure can affect taxes, legal exposure, and post-closing obligations. In a stock sale, the buyer inherits the entire legal entity including anything that was not fully visible during diligence, which makes post-closing disputes and claims more common than in an asset sale. If an LOI proposes a stock sale, it is worth understanding why that structure is being used and how it affects both parties.

## The Purchase Price Is Only a Starting Point

The purchase price in an LOI is typically based on the information available to the buyer at that time.

Due diligence is the process where the buyer verifies that information. If the business performs as represented and the information provided holds up under review, the purchase price often remains unchanged. If material issues are discovered, the buyer may seek adjustments or decide not to proceed.

That does not mean sellers need perfect financial records. Most privately held businesses were built to serve customers and generate profits, not to prepare for an eventual sale. What matters is understanding your numbers well enough to explain them clearly and support them with documentation when requested.

A buyer who encounters an unexpected issue and receives a prompt, well-supported explanation will usually react very differently than a buyer who encounters an issue and receives uncertainty or incomplete information.

## What Is Actually Included in the Purchase Price

One of the most common misconceptions in M&A is that the purchase price equals the amount the seller receives at closing.

In reality, several adjustments often affect the final proceeds.

Many transactions are structured on a cash-free, debt-free basis. Debt-free means existing debt is paid off at or before closing. Cash-free means the seller keeps the cash in the business — but not all of it. Because the business needs to be able to operate normally from the moment ownership changes hands, an agreed-upon amount of cash, receivables, inventory, and other operating assets must remain in the business at closing. That amount is called the working capital target, and it is negotiated and written into the LOI as a specific dollar figure. The seller keeps everything above that figure and takes it out at closing.

Other items may also be negotiated separately, including inventory above the working capital target, accounts receivable, customer deposits, deferred revenue, escrow amounts, and holdbacks.

Understanding exactly what is included in the purchase price before signing an LOI can prevent significant misunderstandings later in the process.

## How the Purchase Price Gets Paid

The purchase price is only one part of the equation. The structure of the payment matters just as much.

Common structures include:

- **Cash at closing.** Some or all of the purchase price is paid when the transaction closes. Generally speaking, the larger the cash-at-closing component, the less risk remains for the seller after the sale.
- **Seller financing.** Part of the purchase price is paid over time through a promissory note from the buyer. In this structure, the seller's full recovery depends on the buyer's ability to continue operating the business successfully.
- **Earnouts.** A portion of the purchase price is contingent on future performance targets. The details matter enormously. Small differences in how targets are defined can dramatically change the outcome.
- **Equity rollovers.** The seller retains partial ownership after closing. This allows the seller to participate in future growth but also leaves some of the value tied to the buyer's future performance.

None of these structures is inherently good or bad. Each reflects a different balance between risk and potential upside.


## Questions Worth Asking Before You Sign

Before signing an LOI, sellers should have reasonable visibility into the following:

- Who will be conducting due diligence and what does that process look like?
- What circumstances could cause the purchase price to change?
- How is the purchase price being paid, and what portion, if any, will be held back after closing?
- How long does exclusivity last, and under what circumstances can it be extended?
- What is the expected timeline to close?
- Is there an outside termination date?
- How does the buyer intend to finance the transaction?

You do not need every answer before signing. You do need enough clarity to avoid major surprises later.

## Don't Overlook Transition Obligations

Purchase price is often the first thing sellers focus on, but many transactions also include obligations that continue after closing.

These may include consulting agreements, transition assistance, non-compete agreements, and non-solicitation agreements. For some sellers, these obligations have a greater day-to-day impact than small differences in purchase price. Understanding what your life looks like after closing is just as important as understanding what your check looks like at closing.

## The Buyer Behind the LOI

Ultimately, an LOI is only the beginning of the process.

The buyer still needs to complete diligence, finalize documentation, secure financing, and close the transaction. What matters most is not simply the offer itself but the quality of the person or organization making it.

The best buyers communicate clearly, move with purpose, ask thoughtful questions, and remain consistent throughout the process. Legitimate discoveries during diligence may require additional discussion, but a disciplined buyer does not use diligence as an excuse to renegotiate every aspect of the deal.

The LOI is not the finish line. It is the starting point. Understanding what you are agreeing to before you sign can make the difference between a smooth closing and a frustrating process that never reaches one.
