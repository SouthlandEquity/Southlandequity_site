You have spent a decade, maybe two or three, building a business worth selling. You have customers who keep coming back, employees who know their jobs, and numbers that tell a real story. By most measures, you are ready.

Then a transaction starts, and it becomes clear that building a strong business and preparing that business for sale are two different things.

The issues that derail deals almost never come from the quality of what was built. They come from a handful of things that running a business day to day never required you to think about. That is not a failure of preparation. It is just how the process works, and knowing what those things are before you are in the middle of a deal is what separates a clean close from a frustrating one.

## How Years of Smart Tax Decisions Can Work Against You in a Sale

Most business owners have spent years running their finances in a way that reduces taxable income. That is a rational way to run a business. The problem is that a buyer is not looking at your financials to understand your tax position. They are looking at them to figure out what the business actually earns, and those two pictures often look very different.

Buyers base their offers on EBITDA, which is shorthand for the operating earnings of the business before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. It is a way of measuring what the business actually generates, stripped of financing decisions and accounting adjustments. Because owners routinely run personal or owner-specific expenses through the business, a buyer will add those back to get a cleaner picture of true earnings. Things like a personal vehicle, family health insurance on the books, or an above-market salary you pay yourself. These are called add-backs, and working through them with a buyer is a normal part of the process.

The problem starts when the add-backs get aggressive.

Every adjustment you claim, a buyer will look at. Some are clean and well-documented. Others invite questions. And some, if they look questionable, do not just get discounted on their own merits. They put everything else in your financials under a harsher light. A buyer who starts wondering whether your numbers are reliable does not keep that doubt confined to one line item. The deal slows down, the offer changes, or it falls apart.

A few that tend to draw scrutiny:

- A vehicle that is on the books as a business expense but is primarily driven by a family member.
- Travel and meals that were loosely categorized as business development but have no real documentation behind them.
- A spouse or family member on payroll in a role that does not reflect what they actually do or what that work would cost on the open market.
- One-time consulting fees paid to a related party, or any arrangement that looks like it was structured to move money rather than pay for a service.

None of these is automatically disqualifying. But a buyer who finds one starts looking harder at everything else. That shift in posture can affect more than just the disputed line item.

There is also a math problem worth understanding. Buyers do not value businesses based on a single year of earnings. They apply a multiplier to a normalized earnings number to arrive at a purchase price. If a disputed add-back reduces that earnings number, even by a modest amount, the impact on your final number is that reduction multiplied out. A $30,000 add-back that gets thrown out does not cost you $30,000. Depending on how the deal was structured, it can cost you several times that.

If a sale is on your horizon in the next few years, a conversation with your accountant now about what your financials will look like to a buyer is time well spent.

## Legal and Structural Issues That Nobody Warned You About

A buyer's attorney is going to go through your business with a level of scrutiny that would feel invasive in any other context. That is not adversarial. It is due diligence, and it is how responsible buyers protect themselves. The question is whether what they find slows things down, changes the deal, or kills it.

A few things that come up more than owners expect:

- **Customer contracts.** How a contract is affected by a sale depends on how the deal is structured. In an asset sale, contracts do not automatically transfer to the buyer. Each one needs to be assigned, which requires the customer's consent. In an equity sale, the buyer steps into your shoes as the owner of the company, so contracts stay in place but any change-of-control clause in the agreement can give the customer the right to exit. Either way, your key customer contracts are worth reviewing before you are in a deal.
- **Lease agreements and real estate.** In an asset sale, your lease does not transfer automatically. The buyer will need to either assume it or negotiate a new one, and the landlord has to agree. In an equity sale, the lease stays with the company, but the landlord may still have approval rights if the agreement has a change-of-control provision. Beyond the transfer question, pay attention to the lease term itself. A lease that is close to expiration gives a buyer legitimate concern about whether the location is secure after the sale. If your lease is up for renewal in the next year or two, that is worth addressing before you go to market. The same applies if you own the real estate separately from the business. Whether that gets sold with the business or stays with you affects the deal structure and how a buyer thinks about ongoing occupancy costs.
- **Employee agreements.** Non-compete and non-solicitation agreements that key employees signed protect the business from those employees leaving and taking customers or colleagues with them. In a sale, a buyer will likely want to know those agreements are in place, enforceable under local law, and actually assignable to a new owner. Separately, any compensation arrangements or equity promises that were made verbally or informally over the years need to be accounted for. Buyers will ask, and undocumented commitments that surface during due diligence create friction.
- **Deferred taxes and liabilities.** How your business is structured and how certain decisions were handled from a tax standpoint will shape what kind of deal structure makes sense and what surprises might be sitting in the books.

None of these issues is automatically a deal-breaker. But each one takes time to work through, and time is not neutral in a transaction. The longer a deal stays open, the more exposure there is to something changing, on either side.

A good business attorney is worth the cost here. Not to complicate the process but to make sure you understand what you are walking into before you are in the middle of it.

## What Happens When the Owner Is the Business

This is probably the most common issue in small business sales, and it is the one that is hardest to see from inside.

If your customers call your cell phone, if your vendors have relationships with you personally rather than with the company, if your team relies on you to make decisions that should have been delegated years ago, then what a buyer is buying is not entirely a standalone business. Part of what they are buying is you. And if you are planning to leave, that creates a real question about what stays behind.

Buyers account for this in different ways. Some will ask for a longer transition period. Some will adjust what they are willing to pay. And some, if the owner-dependence is deep enough, will pass on a business they otherwise liked. This does not mean you need to be invisible in your own company before you sell. It means that the degree to which the business can run without you is a real input into what you can get for it, and it is worth being honest with yourself about where that stands.

## The Expectation Gap

Here is where sellers get hurt the most, and it is almost always avoidable.

A seller hears a number early in a conversation, or reads something about what companies in their industry sell for, and that number gets anchored in their head. Then due diligence happens. Issues surface. Adjustments get made. And what ends up in the final offer looks different than what the seller had been expecting.

If those expectations were set in a casual early conversation and never properly stress-tested, the adjustment feels like a betrayal even when it is not. Deals fall apart over expectation gaps, and both sides lose when that happens.

The honest version of this is: go in with a range, not a number. Understand the general framework for how businesses like yours get valued without locking yourself into a specific figure before a buyer has had the chance to look at everything. A good transaction starts with both sides having realistic expectations, and it is better to arrive there before you are ten weeks into a process.

## Timing Is Part of the Deal

A business that is growing is worth more than a business that just peaked and is starting to slide. That sounds obvious, but the implications are easy to miss.

Sellers sometimes start thinking about a sale after the best years are already behind them. Or they wait until a personal circumstance forces the issue and then try to sell under pressure. Both of those situations compress your options and your leverage.

The window when a business is most attractive to a buyer is when it has momentum, the financials are clean, and the owner is selling from a position of choice rather than necessity. If you are in that window right now, it is worth paying attention to. If you are not there yet, it is worth thinking about what would need to be true before you were.

## What This Is Actually Telling You

None of this is meant to make the process feel impossible. Businesses sell every day, and most of the issues that derail deals are fixable with enough lead time and the right preparation.

What it is meant to flag is that walking into a sale without doing that preparation first can cost you in ways that are hard to recover from, either in price, in deal structure, or in a transaction that never actually closes.

The buyers who are worth working with will tell you the same thing. Getting a deal to close is not just about finding someone who wants what you built. It is about showing up ready for what the process actually requires.
