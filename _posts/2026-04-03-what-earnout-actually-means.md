## The word that makes sellers nervous

If you are talking to a buyer and they mention an earnout, your instinct might be to brace for something that is going to cost you money. That instinct is not entirely wrong — earnouts are often used to bridge a valuation gap, and they carry real risk. But the story is more complicated than that.

An earnout, used in the right situation and structured honestly, can actually work in a seller's favor. Understanding how they work — and when they make sense — is worth the time before you find yourself looking at one in an LOI.

## What an earnout is

An earnout is a provision in a deal structure where a portion of the purchase price is contingent on the business hitting certain performance targets after closing. The buyer pays part of the price at close and agrees to pay additional amounts if the business achieves agreed-upon milestones over a defined period — typically one to three years.

The targets are usually financial. Revenue above a threshold, EBITDA hitting a certain number, a specific customer retention rate. If the business hits the targets, the seller receives the additional payment. If it does not, they do not.

The basic logic from a buyer's perspective is risk mitigation: they are not paying full price for performance that has not yet happened. From a seller's perspective, an earnout can theoretically allow them to capture value for future performance they believe is coming.

## Why sellers often end up disappointed

Here is the honest version of why earnouts have a bad reputation: once a deal closes, the seller loses control of the variables that determine whether the earnout gets paid.

The buyer is now running the business. They make decisions about spending, about sales strategy, about which customers to pursue and which to let go, about how aggressively to invest. Those decisions will affect the financial performance the earnout is measured against. And while most buyers are not running the business to avoid paying an earnout, they are not necessarily running it the way you would have — and those differences can affect the outcome.

There is also the measurement problem. Even straightforward earnout metrics can create disputes about how to calculate them. What counts as revenue? How are shared costs allocated? What happens if a key customer leaves for reasons outside anyone's control? These disputes happen more often than sellers expect, and resolving them can be expensive and adversarial.

The sellers who end up most frustrated with earnouts are usually ones who did not think carefully about the performance metrics, the measurement methodology, and what operational control they would or would not have during the earnout period before they signed.

## When an earnout can genuinely work for a seller

There are situations where an earnout is not just a concession — it is actually the right structure.

The most common one is when there is a real gap between what the business has done historically and what the seller believes it will do in the near future. If you have a major new customer who just signed, or a contract that is closing in ninety days, or a product that is about to launch — and the buyer is understandably skeptical of performance that has not yet shown up in the financials — an earnout lets you say: I am confident enough in what is coming that I will leave money on the table tied to whether it actually happens.

That is a different frame than an earnout that is being used to close a valuation gap the buyer is not sure is real. In the first case, the seller is volunteering to be measured because they believe in the outcome. In the second case, the seller is being asked to take on risk for earnings that may or may not materialize under new ownership.

## What to look for in an earnout if you are considering one

If you are weighing an earnout as part of a deal structure, a few things are worth examining closely before you agree:

How are the targets defined, and how is performance measured? Vague definitions create disputes. The more specifically the targets are written — revenue from which customers, EBITDA calculated how, over exactly which months — the less room there is for disagreement later.

What operational involvement will you have during the earnout period? If you are staying involved and have real influence over the performance the earnout is measuring, the risk looks different than if you are handing over the keys and hoping the buyer runs things the way you would have.

What happens if the buyer makes decisions that affect performance? This is the hard one. If the buyer decides to stop pursuing a certain customer segment, or cuts a sales role, or changes pricing in a way that affects revenue — and those decisions affect the earnout metric — what recourse do you have?

What is the downside if the business underperforms? Understanding the floor matters as much as understanding the ceiling. Make sure the guaranteed portion of the purchase price is one you can live with if the earnout never pays.

## The broader point about deal structure

Earnouts are one piece of a broader conversation about how a deal gets put together. The right structure for any transaction depends on the specific business, the specific buyer, and what both parties are actually trying to achieve.

A seller who understands the pieces — how earnouts work, when they make sense, where the risks are — is in a much better position to evaluate a deal than one who is encountering these terms for the first time across the table from a buyer who has done this before.
