If you have spent any time researching what a business sale looks like, you have probably come across the word "earnout" — usually in a sentence that makes it sound like something to avoid. Sellers who have been through a deal before often say it with a grimace. Advisors warn about it. Articles describe it as a way buyers defer risk onto sellers.

Some of that reputation is deserved. But earnouts are also one of the more misunderstood structures in a business sale, and walking away from one without understanding what it actually is can cost you real money. The same structure that protects a buyer in one situation can meaningfully increase what a seller walks away with in another.

## What an Earnout Is

An earnout is a portion of the purchase price that gets paid out after the sale closes, tied to the future performance of the business. Instead of receiving 100% of the purchase price on closing day, the seller receives a base amount at close and the remainder over time — but only if the business hits certain targets.

Those targets are most commonly financial: revenue, EBITDA, gross profit. But they do not have to be. Some earnouts are tied to operational milestones — landing a specific contract, hitting a customer count, or retaining key employees through a transition period. The metric used should reflect whatever the buyer and seller agree is the most meaningful indicator of whether the business performs as expected.

The earnout period can run anywhere from one year to several years depending on the deal and the nature of what is being measured.

## Why Buyers Use Them

An earnout is not always about a disagreement on value. There are several distinct reasons a buyer might propose one, and understanding which situation you are in matters.

**Bridging a valuation gap.** The most common scenario: the buyer and seller are not aligned on what the business is worth today. The buyer is not willing to pay for performance that has not happened yet. The seller believes it will. The earnout lets both sides proceed while the business itself settles the question over time.

**Reducing upfront exposure on risk they can see.** If a significant portion of revenue runs through one or two customers, if a key contract is up for renewal, or if the business recently hired a new sales team whose pipeline has not yet closed into revenue, a buyer may structure a lower upfront price and attach an earnout. They are not necessarily saying the business is worth less. They are saying they want to share the downside if a specific risk materializes — and share the upside with you if it does not.

**Protecting against something found in due diligence.** If your financials show strong recent growth but the buyer cannot fully verify what drove it, or if there is a concentration issue or a pending operational change, the buyer may use an earnout to put some of that uncertainty back on the seller's side of the ledger rather than absorbing it entirely in the purchase price.

**Retaining the seller through a transition.** Some earnouts are structured less as a valuation tool and more as an incentive to keep the seller engaged and accountable through the handoff period. If a buyer believes a meaningful portion of the business's value is tied to the seller's relationships or operational knowledge, tying part of the payment to a retention period or transition milestone is one way to align both parties through the changeover.

None of these motivations is inherently adversarial. What matters is whether the earnout is structured fairly and whether you understand which scenario you are actually in.

## When an Earnout Works in a Seller's Favor

Whether an earnout works in your favor depends on the structure, not on the fact that one was proposed.

In the valuation gap scenario, an earnout can be the only way to get paid for what your business is actually going to be worth. If you are growing quickly and trailing financials do not capture where revenue is headed, a buyer pricing off historical numbers will come in below what the business will deliver. An earnout tied to forward performance lets you capture that upside if the business performs the way you believe it will.

In the reduced upfront price scenario, the math looks different. The buyer is offering less at close in exchange for a larger potential payout later. Whether that trade makes sense depends on how confident you are in the metrics being used, how much operational control you retain during the earnout period, and whether the total potential payout — base plus earnout — reflects fair value for the business. A lower upfront number is only acceptable if the earnout ceiling is real and reachable.

In either case, the earnout tends to work best when:

- The seller is staying involved in the business for at least part of the earnout period
- The targets are tied to metrics the seller can directly influence
- The measurement methodology is written into the agreement with no ambiguity
- The earnout period is short enough that the seller can reasonably forecast what will happen

That last point matters more than most sellers realize before they are in the middle of a deal. A two-year earnout based on a metric you control is a very different proposition from a four-year earnout based on a metric that depends heavily on what the new owner decides to do with the business.

## When an Earnout Should Make You Cautious

An earnout that looks straightforward at signing can become a genuine problem if the details are not right.

The most common issue is control. Once a business sells, the buyer makes the operating decisions. If your earnout is tied to revenue over the next two years but the new owner decides to raise prices, cut the sales team, or change the go-to-market approach, you may miss your targets for reasons that have nothing to do with the underlying health of the business. If the agreement does not address how major operating decisions get made during the earnout period, you are taking on risk you may not have fully priced.

There are also accounting questions worth taking seriously. EBITDA-based earnouts require agreement on what gets added back and what does not. Revenue-based earnouts require agreement on how revenue is recognized. Retention-based earnouts need clear definitions of what constitutes a qualifying employee and what happens if someone leaves involuntarily. If the methodology is not specified precisely in the purchase agreement, disputes are almost inevitable. This is an area where getting a lawyer involved before you sign anything is not optional.

A few other things to watch for:

- **Earnout caps:** Some agreements cap the total earnout regardless of how the business performs. Know the ceiling before you agree to the structure.
- **Acceleration clauses:** If the business gets sold again or goes through a major structural change during the earnout period, what happens to your payments? This should be addressed explicitly.
- **Integration decisions:** If the buyer plans to fold your business into a larger operation, some of the metrics that made sense as standalone targets may become impossible to track cleanly.

## What the Right Question Is

Before evaluating whether an earnout is acceptable, the more useful question is: what is it solving for in this specific deal? Is the buyer managing a risk they can clearly articulate? Is there a genuine gap between the purchase price and what you believe the business will produce? Is this about keeping you engaged through a transition, or about something they found during due diligence that they have not said plainly?

Each of those situations calls for a different response. Some are worth accepting. Some are worth addressing by providing better documentation or working through the underlying concern directly. Some are legitimate signals that the buyer and seller are not close enough on value for a deal to make sense.

A well-structured earnout in the right situation is not a concession. It is a mechanism that lets both sides share in what happens next. Whether it works for you depends entirely on the details.

## One More Thing Worth Knowing

If you are selling to a buyer who operates without a fund timeline or outside investors, the earnout conversation often looks different than it does with institutional buyers. A permanent holder has no pressure to optimize the structure for a future exit or to compress the upfront number so it looks better in a report. That changes the dynamic, even when the structure itself is the same.

That does not mean earnouts disappear in those conversations. But the motivation behind them tends to be simpler: genuine uncertainty about forward performance or a specific transition risk, not financial engineering.
