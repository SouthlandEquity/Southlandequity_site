For a lot of owners, the financials are the easy part of a sale to think about. The hard part is the people. The woman who has handled your books for twelve years. The guy who built your operations from scratch. The team that showed up every day, grew with you, and made the thing work.

When owners tell us they are nervous about selling, that is usually what is underneath it. Not the price. The people.

This post answers the most common questions owners have about what actually happens to employees when a business changes hands. Some of the answers are reassuring. Some of them require honesty. All of them are worth knowing before you start a conversation with any buyer.

---

## Do buyers have to keep your employees?

In most acquisitions, no. There is no blanket legal requirement that a buyer retain all existing employees after a sale. What happens to your team after a sale is shaped by a lot of things: the type of buyer, the conversations you have with them before closing, the quality and attitude of your employees, whether the business is appropriately staffed, and what your labor costs look like relative to what the business produces. A buyer is going to look at your team the same way they look at everything else in the business. The people who are performing, pulling their weight, and bought into the company's success are assets. Everything else is a cost question they will need to answer on their own terms.

---

## What should you tell your employees, and when?

The short answer is: keep it need-to-know until you and the buyer have agreed on when and how to tell the team. The fewer people who know a sale is in process, the lower the risk that something leaks before you are ready to address it. There may be a point where you need to bring in a specific employee to help locate or pull records, and that is fine. Bring in who you need, when you need them, and keep the circle tight until you are ready to communicate broadly.

There is also a practical reality that does not get discussed enough. If employees find out a sale is being discussed and the deal ultimately falls through, you could find yourself in a worse position than before the process started. Key people may start quietly looking elsewhere. Uncertainty spreads faster than facts. A business that was healthy going into the process can come out of it with real damage if word got out too early and the deal did not close.

That said, once you are at a point where closing is likely, working through the timing and approach with your buyer is genuinely important. Some buyers prefer that employees know sooner rather than later. They want to start building trust with the team before the ink is dry, address uncertainty directly, and avoid anyone feeling blindsided. Others prefer to wait until closing day and be standing in the room when the news is delivered, so the message is controlled and there is someone there to answer questions on the spot.

Both approaches work. Neither is wrong. What matters is that you and your buyer think it through together before it happens rather than improvising in the moment.

---

## What happens to employees on closing day?

The answer depends on whether the deal is structured as an asset acquisition or a stock acquisition, and the difference is meaningful for your team.

In an asset acquisition, which is the more common structure for this type of transaction, the buyer is purchasing the assets of the business rather than the legal entity itself. Because they are not acquiring the entity that employs your people, the existing employment relationships are technically ended and new ones are created with the buyer. Employees are terminated by the selling entity and rehired by the new owner. That does not mean anyone actually loses their job in a practical sense, but it does mean the legal slate is wiped clean. Prior employment agreements, benefit plan participation, and anything tied to tenure with the old entity does not automatically follow them to the new one.

In a stock acquisition, the buyer purchases the business entity itself. The legal employer does not change, and employees remain employed under the same terms without a technical break in service.

Which structure applies to your deal has real implications for your team and is worth understanding before you get to that point.

---

## What about benefits, PTO, and seniority?

Almost always, yes, these change in an asset acquisition. Because employment technically starts fresh with the new owner, accrued PTO, benefit plan participation, and recognized tenure do not automatically carry over unless the buyer specifically agrees to honor them.

In a stock acquisition the situation is different, since the legal employer and existing employment terms remain in place. But asset deals are far more common, and sellers are often surprised to learn that things they assumed would transfer simply do not.

If you have long-tenured employees who have built up significant PTO or have been operating under informal arrangements, surface those early. They become complications if a buyer discovers them late rather than having them disclosed upfront.

---

## Will your key people be asked to stay?

Almost always, yes. Buyers want the people who know how the business actually runs.

The buyer has an interest in keeping institutional knowledge intact, and a well-run business with stable leadership is worth more to them than one that loses its key people on day one. That said, a buyer may not fully know what changes they want to make until they have personally met your employees and seen how they respond to new ownership. Some people will embrace it. Some will not. That reality will shape decisions that no one can predict before the deal closes.

If there are two or three people whose departure would genuinely put the business at risk, that is worth being honest about with a prospective buyer. It is relevant to how they think about the deal.

---

## What if a buyer wants to make changes after the sale?

They likely will. Any buyer who tells you nothing will ever change is either uninformed or not being straight with you.

What matters is the nature and pace of the changes. A buyer who sees your team as part of what makes the business worth buying is going to approach changes differently than one who views the acquisition primarily as a financial transaction. The first kind of buyer wants the people to succeed. The second kind is optimizing for a different outcome.

That distinction is worth probing in your conversations with buyers. How do they talk about your employees? Do they ask about the people by name? Do they seem to understand what different people actually do? Or does the conversation stay at the level of headcount and payroll costs?

---

## How does buyer type affect what happens to employees?

This is one of the most important and least-discussed parts of the decision to sell.

A permanent buyer, one who intends to own and operate the business indefinitely rather than resell it, has a fundamentally different relationship with your employees than a buyer who plans to hold the business for three to five years and sell again. The permanent buyer needs those people to stay and grow with the business. The timeline buyer may need them to stay long enough to hit certain targets before the next transaction.

Neither approach is dishonest. But the implications for your team are different, and you should understand what kind of buyer you are talking to before you get attached to a number.

A direct buyer who uses their own capital and has no outside investors to answer to is also in a different position than a buyer who needs to produce returns for a fund. When the capital structure changes, the pressure changes. That pressure finds its way to employees one way or another over time.

---

## Can you negotiate employee protections into the deal?

Most things in a deal are negotiable to some degree. But there is something worth understanding before you push hard on any of them: a business is worth what it is worth. If you negotiate commitments that add costs the buyer did not underwrite when they valued the deal, those costs have to come from somewhere. In most cases they come out of your purchase price. And because acquisitions are typically priced as a multiple of earnings, every dollar of added annual cost does not reduce your price by a dollar. It reduces it by that amount multiplied by the multiple. A seemingly modest concession can have a meaningfully larger impact on what you actually walk away with than it appears on the surface.

It is also worth being realistic about what any buyer is actually agreeing to when they commit to keeping employees. No buyer is going to put themselves in a position where they are obligated to retain someone who is underperforming or someone who is openly resentful that the business changed hands. That is not a reasonable ask, and most sellers would not have tolerated it in their own business either. What you are really looking for is a buyer who genuinely wants your good people to stay, and that comes through in the conversation long before anything is written down.

The most durable protection for your employees is not a clause in an agreement. It is choosing the right buyer to begin with.

---

## A final thought

If your employees are the reason this decision is hard, that is not something to push past. It is actually a good sign. It means you have built something that matters to more than just you.

The right buyer understands that. They will not ask you to set it aside.
