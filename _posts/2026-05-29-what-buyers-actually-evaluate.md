You have spent years running your business, and if you are thinking about selling, you have probably spent some time thinking about what it is worth. Maybe you have a number in your head. Maybe someone gave you a ballpark at a golf outing or a chamber lunch.

What most owners have not thought through is what a buyer is actually looking at when they sit down with your financials, walk your facility, and start asking questions. Valuation matters, but the evaluation that produces it is more nuanced than a multiple applied to last year's earnings. Understanding how a serious buyer looks at a business before you go to market is one of the more useful things you can do to prepare.

Here is what is actually on the list.

## The Financial Picture, Beneath the Surface

Buyers start with the financials, and they are not just reading the totals. They are tracing how money moves through the business: where revenue comes from, how reliably it comes in, and what it actually costs to generate it.

A few things get close attention:

- **Revenue concentration.** If one customer represents a significant share of your revenue, buyers will ask hard questions about what happens if that relationship changes. This is not a deal-killer on its own, but it is a risk that gets priced in. The same logic applies to a single supplier, a single geographic market, or a single product line that carries the whole business.
- **Recurring versus one-time revenue.** Revenue that comes back without having to re-sell it every cycle is worth more than revenue that has to be won fresh each year. Contract structures, repeat customer rates, and subscription arrangements all matter here.
- **Margin consistency.** A business that has earned similar margins across multiple years tells a story about operational discipline. One that has swung around a lot prompts questions about why.
- **Add-backs and adjustments.** When sellers present adjusted EBITDA, buyers expect to scrutinize every adjustment. Some add-backs are standard and well-supported. Others, if they look questionable, do not just get discounted. They raise broader doubts about the integrity of the financials. Running personal or borderline expenses through the business has a real cost in a sale context: it is not just the amount of the add-back that is at risk. It is that amount multiplied by the purchase multiple, or in some cases, the deal itself.

## What Keeps the Business Running Without You

This is where many owner-run businesses face their hardest questions. If the owner is the business, a buyer is not acquiring a company. They are acquiring a dependency.

Buyers want to understand the degree to which the business functions at a high level when the owner steps back. That does not mean you need to be fully removed already. But it does mean buyers will want to see:

- A management team or key employees who carry real operational responsibility
- Customer relationships that belong to the company, not just to you personally
- Processes and systems that are documented and repeatable, not stored in one person's head
- Institutional knowledge that can transfer

None of this is a disqualifier if it is not already in place. A good buyer accounts for transition and helps build what is missing. But it will shape how they think about risk, and risk shapes price.

## The Customers Themselves

Beyond revenue concentration, buyers want to understand the nature of your customer relationships. How long have your top customers been with you? Is there a contract, or is the relationship purely relationship-based? Have there been any recent losses or meaningful shifts in how key customers are buying from you?

This is also where buyers will look at whether the business is growing the customer base, holding steady, or slowly concentrating into fewer and fewer accounts. Trends matter as much as snapshots.

## The People

If you have good people, that is real value, and a serious buyer will recognize it. They will ask about tenure, roles, compensation, and whether key employees are likely to stay through and after a transition.

They are not just looking for warm bodies. They are looking for whether the team around you makes the business more or less dependent on any single person, including you. Strong, stable teams compress risk. High turnover or an organization that is thin below the owner level adds it back.

## The Market the Business Operates In

Buyers will form a view on the broader competitive environment your business sits inside. Is the market you serve growing, stable, or contracting? Do you hold a clear position in it, or is the business competing mostly on price?

You do not need to have a formal competitive analysis in hand, but you should be able to speak fluently to who your competitors are, how you win business, and what would have to change for a competitor to take meaningful share from you. A buyer who cannot get a clear picture here will fill in the gaps with assumptions, and those assumptions will not favor you.

## The Real Estate and Physical Assets

If the business owns or leases a facility, buyers will look at the lease terms, the condition of the space, and whether the location is an asset or a constraint. If the business operates out of a building you personally own, that is a separate transaction that needs to be addressed, and getting ahead of it makes the process cleaner.

Equipment, vehicles, and other physical assets will get looked at too. Not necessarily with a fine-tooth comb, but a buyer wants to know whether the assets are maintained or whether there is a capital catch-up expense sitting right behind the sale.

## Legal and Compliance History

A clean legal picture matters. Buyers will look for pending litigation, regulatory exposure, environmental issues, and anything that looks like an ongoing liability. Most small businesses do not have significant issues here, but surprises in this area are the kind that can slow or derail a deal at a late stage. Knowing your own exposure before a buyer finds it is a better position to be in.

## Owner Transition and Timeline

Buyers want to understand not just what the business is, but what the handoff looks like. Are you willing to stay involved for a transition period? What does that look like practically? Do you have employees who can step into roles you currently fill, or does a successful transition depend heavily on your continued presence?

There are no universal right answers here. What buyers want to see is that you have thought through this honestly and that there is a workable path. An owner who has not considered the transition at all is harder to work with than one who has strong preferences about it.

## What a Serious Buyer Does With All of This

A buyer who has done this before is not running through a checklist to find problems. They are building a picture of a business so they can make a confident offer and own it well after close. Most of what gets discovered in a well-run due diligence process is not disqualifying. It is clarifying. It shapes terms, informs how the transition gets structured, and identifies what needs attention after close.

The businesses that move cleanly through a sale process are not necessarily the ones with no issues. They are the ones where the owner understands their own business clearly enough to present it honestly and answer questions directly. That kind of fluency builds trust, and trust moves deals forward.
