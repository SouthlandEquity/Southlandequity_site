You probably did not wake up one morning and decide today was the day. That is not how it works for most owners. The thought comes in pieces. A long drive home from the office. A conversation with your spouse that lingers. A moment where you catch yourself doing the math and then immediately feel guilty about it.

That is normal. And it does not mean anything is wrong with you or with the business. It means you have built something worth thinking seriously about.

What follows are five signs that might indicate you are further along in that thinking than you have let yourself admit.

## 1. You Have Started Mentally Stepping Back

There is a version of stepping back that is strategic and healthy, where you are building systems and putting the right people in place so the business runs without you in every conversation. That kind of stepping back makes the business more valuable and more resilient.

There is another version that feels different. Where you catch yourself less curious about what is happening day to day. Where the problems that used to fire you up now just feel like problems. Where you show up and do the work, but the pull that was always there has gotten quieter.

If you are honest with yourself and it is the second version, that is worth paying attention to. It does not mean you have failed or that you owe the business a different version of yourself. It may mean you have simply given what you had to give, and the business might be better served by someone who is earlier in that arc.

## 2. You Have No Obvious Successor Inside the Business

A lot of owners assume the exit question and the succession question are the same question. They are related, but they are not identical.

If you have a son, daughter, or longtime manager who is ready and willing to take the business over, that is one path. But if you look at the people around you and none of them are positioned or interested in owning what you have built, that is useful information.

It does not automatically mean selling is the answer. But it does mean that if you were to step away without a plan, the thing you spent the better part of your career building would be left in an uncertain position. Some owners sit with that reality for years before doing anything about it. The earlier you look at it clearly, the more options you have.

## 3. Growth Would Require More Than You Want to Give

There is a version of your business that could be significantly larger. Maybe it would require a capital investment you do not want to make right now. Maybe it would require hiring and managing a bigger team, opening a second location, or entering markets you have not pursued. Maybe it would just require another decade of the same level of intensity you have already been running at.

None of that is wrong. But if the path to the next level asks something of you that you are not interested in giving anymore, that tension is worth examining.

A buyer who comes in with operating experience and a genuine interest in what you have built may be better positioned to take the business where it could go. You would capture the value of what you have already created, and the business would have a real shot at the growth it is capable of.

## 4. You Have Become the Single Point of Failure

Most owners understand intellectually that being irreplaceable is not the same thing as being valuable. A business that cannot function without the owner in the building is harder to sell, harder to scale, and a harder thing to ever take a real vacation from.

If your name is on every relationship, if the key employees look to you for decisions that should be made at a lower level, if the business would lose momentum quickly without you present, that is not a reason to avoid selling. But it is something a serious buyer will think about, and it is something that affects how a transition actually goes.

Some owners fix this before going to market. Others find a buyer willing to take it on as it is, often because the buyer intends to be operationally involved. Either way, recognizing it clearly is the first step.

## 5. You Keep Running the Numbers

At some point you start doing the math in your head. Not a spreadsheet with assumptions and scenarios, just a ballpark sense of what the business might be worth and what you could do with that number.

That mental math is not greed and it is not disloyalty to the business. It is your brain telling you that you have built something with real value and that you are allowed to think about what comes next for you.

The owners who wait the longest are often the ones who feel like thinking about selling is a betrayal of the thing they built. It is not. Taking what you created seriously enough to think about its future, including a future where you are not running it, is one of the more honest things you can do.

---

None of these signs mean you should sell tomorrow, or that you should sell at all. What they mean is that the question deserves more than a passing thought. And the sooner you start thinking about it clearly, the more options you will have when you are ready to act.
