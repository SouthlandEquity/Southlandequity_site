## The number in your head is probably not the market number

If you have owned your business for fifteen or twenty years, you have a number in your head. It might be based on what a peer sold for. It might be based on what you would need to retire comfortably. It might be based on something you read once about how businesses in your industry are valued.

That number may or may not reflect what a buyer would actually pay. Most often, sellers are either higher or lower than market — and arriving at the conversation without understanding why can make the whole process harder than it needs to be.

This is not a knock on business owners. Valuation is legitimately complicated, and there is no single right answer. But there are frameworks that most buyers use, and understanding them before you start talking to anyone puts you in a much stronger position.

## The most common valuation method: EBITDA multiples

The foundation of most small business valuations is a multiple applied to EBITDA — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Buyers calculate EBITDA as a proxy for the cash the business generates, then apply a multiple to arrive at an enterprise value.

The multiple is not fixed — it varies by industry, by business size, by growth trajectory, by customer concentration, by how dependent the business is on the owner, and by market conditions at the time of the transaction. A business generating $500,000 in EBITDA might trade at four to six times. A business generating $2 million in EBITDA in a growing industry with a strong management team might trade at six to eight times or higher.

Size matters more than many sellers expect. Buyers price smaller businesses lower on a multiple basis, in part because smaller businesses carry more concentration risk — one customer, one key employee, one supplier — and in part because there are fewer potential buyers competing for them. As EBITDA grows, multiples tend to expand.

## What adjusted EBITDA means — and why it matters

Your reported EBITDA on the tax return is almost never the number a buyer uses. Most closely-held businesses have expenses running through them that would not exist under new ownership: above-market owner compensation, personal vehicle expenses, meals and entertainment, a family member on payroll, rent paid to a related party at above-market rates.

A buyer will work with you to add those back to get to what is sometimes called adjusted EBITDA or seller's discretionary earnings — the real economic earnings of the business when you remove expenses that exist because the owner controls both sides of the ledger.

This is legitimate and expected. But the more unusual or aggressive the add-backs are, the more friction they create. A buyer who cannot independently verify that an expense is genuinely owner-specific will discount it or push back. Simple, well-documented add-backs are better than complex ones.

## Other methods you might hear about

The EBITDA multiple approach is not the only way businesses get valued, though it is the most common for operating companies.

Revenue multiples are sometimes used in industries where EBITDA is compressed or negative — technology, some service businesses early in their lifecycle. But for most of the established, profitable Georgia businesses that change hands, EBITDA is the primary lens.

Asset-based valuations matter most when the business has significant tangible assets — real estate, equipment, fleet — that could be sold independently. For service businesses or businesses where the value is in the customer relationships and cash flow rather than hard assets, asset value is typically a floor, not the primary driver.

Comparable transactions are useful reference points. What did similar businesses in your industry sell for? A good advisor can pull transaction data and give you a sense of where your business fits relative to recent deals.

## What moves the multiple up or down

Understanding what drives the multiple is more useful than memorizing the average. A few things that tend to push value higher:

Recurring or contractual revenue that does not need to be re-earned from scratch every year. A strong management team that could run the business without the owner. Low customer concentration — no single customer more than fifteen or twenty percent of revenue. Clean, consistent financials. A defensible market position. Geographic diversification or multiple service lines. A long customer tenure that signals real loyalty, not just price sensitivity.

Things that tend to move value lower: heavy owner-dependence, concentrated customer risk, inconsistent financials, aging equipment or infrastructure that needs capital investment, a declining market, or a business model that is vulnerable to disruption.

## Getting a number before you need one

A lot of business owners wait until they are ready to sell to find out what their business is worth. That is understandable, but it creates problems. You might discover the value is lower than you need to fund retirement — which means you are now in a time-pressured situation where you need the business to grow. You might discover the value is much higher than you expected and realize you could have sold three years ago. Either way, finding out late limits your options.

Getting a rough valuation two or three years before you plan to sell — either through a formal valuation or a candid conversation with a buyer who is willing to be honest with you — gives you time to make decisions with better information. You can invest in reducing owner-dependence. You can work on customer concentration. You can clean up the financials. All of those things take time to show up in the numbers.

The best time to understand what your business is worth is before you have to know.
