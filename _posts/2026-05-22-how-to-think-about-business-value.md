You have spent years building something real. At some point, when the idea of selling starts to feel less hypothetical, one of the first things you want to know is what the business is worth.

That question is reasonable. But the way sellers usually approach it, and the way buyers actually answer it, are often two different things. At the most basic level, buyers arrive at a purchase price by looking at what a business earns and multiplying that number by some factor based on the risk and opportunity they see. The multiple applied to those earnings can move significantly depending on what a buyer finds when they look closely at the business. Understanding that framework before you have your first conversation with a buyer puts you in a fundamentally better position.

## Buyers Are Not Buying What You Built. They Are Buying What It Produces.

This is the hardest shift for most owners to make, and it is not a criticism of what they have built. It is just how acquisitions work.

What you have built is a company with history, relationships, reputation, and people who have shown up every day for years. That matters. But when a buyer looks at your business, the first thing they are trying to understand is the earnings the business generates on a recurring, sustainable basis, and how those earnings are likely to hold up after you are no longer running things.

The metric buyers most commonly use for this is EBITDA, which stands for earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. It is a way of looking at what the business earns from its operations before accounting for how it is financed or how its assets are structured. It is not a perfect number, but it is the common language of business acquisitions, so it is worth understanding before you walk into any conversation.

Buyers rarely take EBITDA at face value. They go through the financials and make adjustments in both directions. Expenses that would not exist under new ownership get removed. Costs the business should be carrying but isn't get added in. The resulting number after all of those adjustments is called adjusted EBITDA, and it is what most offers are actually based on. Some of those adjustments work in your favor. Some do not.

## The Adjustments That Work in Your Favor

Personal expenses that have been run through the business are the most common favorable add-back. A personal vehicle expensed to the company, personal travel blended in with business travel, and one-time costs that are not expected to recur all reduce your stated earnings but would not be costs a new owner carries. Adding them back shows a higher, more accurate picture of what the business actually produces. If the current owner is taking above-market compensation, a buyer will also normalize that down to what a replacement would cost, which reduces expenses and increases adjusted earnings.

Your accountant can help you identify what is defensible. The goal is to show a buyer the true recurring cost structure of the business under new ownership. Keep in mind that every add-back you present will be scrutinized. If one cannot be clearly justified, a buyer will push back on it, and since your offer is based on a multiple of adjusted earnings, losing one dollar of EBITDA costs you several dollars of purchase price. Aggressive or unclear add-backs can also create doubt about the integrity of the financials overall, and that doubt is hard to recover from once it is in a buyer's head.

## The Adjustments That Work Against You

Just as a buyer removes expenses that would not exist under new ownership, they also add in costs that the business should be carrying but currently is not. These adjustments reduce adjusted EBITDA rather than increase it. Common examples include:

- A below-market lease with a renewal coming up post-close
- Compensation that is below what a replacement would cost
- Deferred maintenance on equipment or facilities
- Underinsured or uninsured risks
- A role the current owner fills personally that a new owner would need to hire for

The practical point is not that you need to change how the business operates before a sale. It is that knowing where these adjustments are likely to land gives you a clearer sense of what your number actually looks like to a buyer before you sit down with one.

## What Drives the Multiple

Adjusted EBITDA is the base. The multiple applied to it is where most of the variance in business value comes from, and that multiple is not random. Buyers are pricing the risk they are taking on, and one of the most consistent drivers of that multiple is simply the size of the business. Smaller businesses tend to trade at lower multiples than larger ones because the risk is more concentrated. There are typically fewer people, fewer customer relationships, and less margin for error if something goes wrong. As earnings grow, multiples generally expand because a buyer sees a business that is less dependent on any one thing going right.

A few things that push a multiple higher:

- **Revenue is recurring or predictable.** Contracts, repeat customers, and consistent demand are easier to underwrite than project-based or one-time revenue.
- **Customer concentration is low.** If no single customer accounts for a large share of revenue, the business does not go sideways if one relationship ends.
- **The business does not run on you.** If there is a management team and operational structure that functions without you in the building every day, a buyer can see themselves owning it successfully.
- **Growth has been consistent.** A business that has grown steadily over several years is easier to justify paying more for than one with volatile results.

A few things that push it lower:

- **Owner dependency is high.** If you hold the key customer relationships, are the primary salesperson, or are the only person who knows how certain things work, a buyer is underwriting the risk that those things do not transfer cleanly.
- **Customer concentration is high.** If one or a handful of customers account for a large share of revenue, the business is fragile in a way that a buyer has to price in.
- **The business is capital intensive.** EBITDA adds back depreciation, but depreciation represents real equipment that has to be replaced. In businesses that require significant ongoing capital expenditure just to maintain operations, buyers will discount the multiple because a portion of those earnings is not freely available.
- **Results are inconsistent.** A business with wide swings in revenue or earnings is harder to value with confidence.
- **The industry is in structural decline.** Buyers factor in where a business is headed, not just where it has been.

None of these factors are things you can fully control, but knowing which ones apply to your business gives you a clearer picture of how a buyer is going to see it, and whether there are things worth addressing before you start a process.

## The Difference Between Value and Price

A business does not have a single, correct value. It has a value to a particular buyer, in a particular set of circumstances, at a particular moment.

A buyer who plans to hold a business for the long term and keep running it is going to see it differently than one who intends to merge it into something else, cut overhead, and sell it again in a few years. The assumptions each buyer makes about what the business looks like in their hands will drive their offer, and those assumptions can vary widely.

This is why the kind of buyer you are talking to matters as much as the number they put in front of you. Two offers at the same price can lead to very different outcomes depending on what the buyer intends to do with the business. That question is worth asking directly before you get too deep into any conversation.

## Getting a Realistic Baseline

Before you talk to a buyer, you should have a rough sense of where you stand. That does not require commissioning a formal valuation. What it does require is having clean financial statements, understanding what your adjusted EBITDA looks like over the last several years, and having an honest conversation with yourself about the factors that a buyer is going to see as risk.

If your financials are a mess or your add-backs are going to be hard to defend, that is not a reason to avoid a sale. It is useful information that lets you decide whether to clean things up first, price your expectations accordingly, or simply know what you are walking into.

The sellers who have the best experiences in this process are usually the ones who go in with clear eyes about what they have, not the ones who go in with the highest expectations.
