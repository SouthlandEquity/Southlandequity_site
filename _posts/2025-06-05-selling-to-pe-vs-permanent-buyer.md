You have probably heard the term private equity enough times that it starts to feel like the default answer when someone asks who buys businesses. That is understandable. PE firms are loud, well-funded, and in many cases the first buyer type that shows up when you start poking around. But there is another category of buyer that operates very differently, and if you are thinking about selling, the contrast is worth understanding before you get to the table.

This is not about one being good and the other being bad. It is about how each type of buyer is structured, what they are optimizing for, and what that means for you, your people, and the business you are handing over.

## How Private Equity Is Built

Private equity firms raise money from outside investors, typically institutional players like pension funds, endowments, and family offices. That capital gets pooled into a fund with a fixed life, usually somewhere in the range of seven to ten years. The firm has a legal obligation to return capital to those investors within that window, which means every business they acquire needs to be sold again before the clock runs out.

That structure shapes everything. The firm is not buying your business to run it indefinitely. They are buying it to grow it, often through some combination of operational improvement and additional acquisitions, and then sell it at a higher number than they paid. The exit is built into the model from the start.

None of that is hidden. PE firms will tell you this clearly if you ask. The question is what it means for you and your business in the years between signing and that next sale.

## What the Fund Clock Changes

When a buyer has a hard deadline to exit, a few things tend to follow.

**A second sale is coming.** At some point, usually within a few years, your business goes back to market. That means your employees, your management team, and your customers go through another ownership transition. Some businesses handle that fine. Others find it disruptive. It depends on how the business is run during the hold period and who ends up buying it the second time.

**Decisions get made with the exit in mind.** Because that second sale is the goal, capital allocation, hiring, systems, and pricing strategy all get evaluated against how they look to a future buyer. That is not always misaligned with running a good business, but it introduces a filter that did not exist when you were running the company for the long term.

**The clock creates an incentive to cut.** This is the part that does not always get said plainly. When a buyer needs to show strong earnings within a fixed window, the fastest way to do that is often to reduce costs: headcount, reinvestment, maintenance, anything that hits the bottom line in the short term. Those cuts can make the financials look better heading into a sale while quietly eroding the things that made the business worth buying in the first place. Long-term contracts get deprioritized. Equipment that should be replaced gets pushed another year. People who carry institutional knowledge walk out the door. The business that gets sold the second time can look very different from the one you handed over.

**The people around the deal change over time.** The partner who shook your hand at close may not be the one managing the relationship in year four. Funds bring in operating teams, hire new management, and turn over personnel across a hold period. The continuity you were told to expect during diligence is often a function of whoever is assigned to your business at a given moment.

Again: private equity acquires a lot of businesses and plenty of those deals work out well for everyone involved. The point is not that the model is flawed. The point is that it is a specific model with a specific structure, and if you are selling, you should understand exactly what you are agreeing to.

## What a Permanent Buyer Looks Like

A permanent buyer is one who acquires a business with the intention of holding it indefinitely and growing its value over the long term. The decisions they make are oriented around what the business looks like in ten or twenty years, not what it looks like to a buyer in five. That difference in time horizon is what separates them from a PE firm, and it runs through everything.

A PE firm is optimizing for exit value. Every dollar they put into the business, every hire they make, every operational change they push through gets evaluated against how it affects the sale they are already planning. That is not irrational. It is exactly what their model requires. But it means the business is being managed toward a finish line that has nothing to do with its long-term health.

A permanent buyer is not managing toward a finish line. They are building something they intend to keep. Capital goes in based on what makes the business stronger over time. The management team they commit to is the one they are counting on for the long haul. And the decisions that get made in year three are made by the same owner who signed the purchase agreement, not by whoever the fund assigned to the account that quarter.



## What Sellers Actually Care About

When you talk to owners who have gone through a sale, the financial terms matter, of course. But what tends to surface most in conversations after the fact is everything that happened next.

Did the buyer treat the management team the way they said they would? Did they keep the commitments they made, even the informal ones? Did the business hold together, or did it get carved up and reorganized into something unrecognizable? Did the employees who had been there for twenty years still feel like they had a place?

A permanent buyer does not have a guarantee on any of those things. What they do have is a direct stake in the outcome that does not expire. They are not handing the business off to someone else in five years. The decisions they make land on them.

## The Credibility Question

Permanent buyers, particularly smaller or independent ones, are sometimes written off as less serious than institutional buyers. The assumption is that without a formal fund structure, they lack the capital, the process, or the sophistication to close a deal efficiently.

That assumption does not hold up when you look at it carefully.

The ability to close a deal is a function of capital access, legal and financial counsel, and whether the buyer knows how to move through diligence without introducing unnecessary delays. None of those things are exclusive to institutional buyers. A permanent buyer who has built and sold businesses, who is buying with their own capital, and who has experienced advisors around them can execute as cleanly as anyone. Often more cleanly, because there is no investment committee to satisfy and no approval chain to navigate.



## Thinking Through the Tradeoffs

Neither structure is right for every situation. There are businesses well-suited for private equity, particularly those positioned for aggressive growth or where the owner wants a clean exit and is comfortable with a subsequent sale down the road. There are also businesses where permanent ownership matters more than the highest number on the page, where the culture is fragile enough that another transition in three years would do real damage, or where the owner cares enough about the people they are leaving behind to weight that heavily in who they sell to.

The right buyer depends on what you are actually trying to accomplish. That includes what you want for the business, what you want for your team, and what you want to feel when you look back at the decision five years from now.

Understanding the structural differences between buyer types is the first step in asking the right questions. Not just "what will they pay?" but "what happens after they pay it?"

That second question is usually where the real answer lives.
