Most business owners spend years building something they are proud of. When it finally comes time to sell, the last thing they expect is a phone call a week before closing telling them the purchase price just changed. That call usually involves two words: working capital.

## What Is Working Capital?

Working capital is the difference between your current assets — cash, accounts receivable, inventory — and your current liabilities — accounts payable, accrued expenses, short-term debt. At its core, it is a measure of the liquidity available to run the business on a day-to-day basis.

When a buyer acquires a business, they are not just buying the brand, the customer relationships, and the equipment. They are also buying the operational engine that keeps the business running. That engine needs fuel, and working capital is that fuel.

## Why Does It Come Up at Closing?

The purchase price in your Letter of Intent is typically set assuming the business will be delivered with a "normal" level of working capital. That normal level — called the working capital target or peg — is usually calculated as a trailing average of the past twelve months.

If you close the deal with more working capital than the peg, you may receive a dollar-for-dollar adjustment upward. If you close with less, you will owe the buyer the shortfall.

## What Sellers Get Wrong

The most common mistake sellers make is treating the period between signing and closing as a time to pull cash out of the business. Accelerating collections, delaying payments to vendors, or drawing down inventory all look attractive in the short run. But each of those actions reduces working capital, and that reduction shows up directly in the final settlement statement.

A second mistake is failing to understand what is and is not included in the working capital calculation. Cash is often excluded. Certain accruals may or may not be included depending on how the purchase agreement defines current liabilities. These definitions matter enormously and deserve careful attention before you sign.

## How to Protect Yourself

The best thing you can do is get involved in the working capital conversation early. Before you sign an LOI, ask the buyer how they intend to define and calculate the peg. Make sure your accountant reviews the methodology. And run the business normally through closing — resist the temptation to optimize short-term cash at the expense of the working capital balance.
