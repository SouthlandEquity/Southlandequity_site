## The LOI is not the deal — but it shapes everything that comes after

When a buyer sends you a letter of intent — sometimes called an LOI — it can feel like the hard part is over. You have found someone who wants to buy, you have agreed on a general price range, and the rest is paperwork.

That is not quite right. The LOI is a summary of the major deal terms that the parties intend to formalize in a purchase agreement. Most of its terms are not legally binding — meaning the buyer can still walk away, and so can you. But the terms in the LOI become the framework for everything that follows: the due diligence process, the final purchase agreement, the negotiations over every point that was not spelled out clearly.

Getting into the LOI without understanding what you are agreeing to — and what you are not — can create serious problems later in the process.

## Price is usually a range, not a number

Most LOIs describe a purchase price in terms of a formula rather than a fixed dollar amount. The price is typically expressed as a multiple of EBITDA, subject to a quality of earnings review. What that means in practice is that the final price can shift based on what the buyer finds during due diligence.

If the buyer's due diligence turns up earnings that are lower than what was represented, or add-backs that do not hold up to scrutiny, the price will be adjusted downward. That is a legitimate part of the process. What sellers sometimes do not anticipate is how material that adjustment can be — and how little leverage they have to push back on it once due diligence is underway and the buyer has invested significant time and money.

Before you sign the LOI, understand how the price is calculated and what the realistic range of outcomes looks like based on your actual financials.

## Working capital is one of the most negotiated points — and the most misunderstood

Almost every LOI includes a working capital provision. Working capital, in broad terms, is the current assets of the business minus its current liabilities — the cash and near-cash resources required to run the business day-to-day.

The reason this comes up at closing is that buyers and sellers often have different intuitions about what should be included in the purchase price. A seller typically wants to take as much cash out of the business as possible before closing. A buyer typically needs adequate working capital left in the business to keep it operating smoothly after close.

The LOI will usually specify a target working capital amount — what the buyer expects to receive at closing — and the purchase price will be adjusted up or down based on whether actual working capital at close is above or below that target. Sellers who do not understand this provision sometimes end up surprised that cash they expected to receive at closing is being credited back to the buyer as a working capital adjustment.

This is worth understanding clearly before you sign, and worth having an advisor explain in the context of your specific business.

## What exclusivity means

Most LOIs include an exclusivity provision — a period of time during which you agree not to talk to other potential buyers while the current buyer completes due diligence. This period is typically sixty to ninety days, though it can be longer.

Exclusivity is reasonable from a buyer's perspective. They are about to invest significant time and money in your business, and they do not want to do that while you are shopping the business to someone else. But it means that if the deal falls apart at the end of the due diligence period — for any reason — you have lost sixty to ninety days and will be starting over with whatever buyer interest remains.

The practical implication: make sure the buyer is serious before you grant exclusivity. Have a real conversation about their capital source, their timeline, and their prior experience closing deals. A buyer who has done this before will not object to that conversation.

## The binding provisions matter

While most of an LOI is non-binding, some provisions are binding from the moment you sign. These typically include the exclusivity period, confidentiality obligations, and any break-up fees or expense reimbursement provisions.

Read these carefully. If the LOI includes a break-up fee — money you owe the buyer if you walk away from the deal — understand under what circumstances it applies. These provisions are negotiable, and it is easier to negotiate them before you sign than after.

## Due diligence is longer than you think

The period between signing the LOI and reaching a final purchase agreement — when the buyer is conducting due diligence and the lawyers are drafting documents — is typically longer and more demanding than sellers expect. For a small to mid-size business, sixty to ninety days is common. It can run longer for complex businesses or if issues surface during the process.

During that time, you will be providing financial records, customer contracts, employee agreements, lease documents, insurance policies, and anything else the buyer asks for. You will be doing this while running your business. Most sellers describe this period as exhausting.

Plan for it before you sign the LOI. Make sure you have advisors in place — a lawyer and an accountant who have been through this before — before the clock starts.

## The LOI is also where you learn the most about the buyer

How a buyer handles the LOI process tells you a lot about how they will handle the rest of the deal. A buyer who is organized, communicates clearly, and has done this before will produce a clean LOI and be willing to walk through every term with you. A buyer who is vague, slow, or evasive on the front end tends to stay that way.

Pay attention to what the LOI process feels like, not just what it says.
