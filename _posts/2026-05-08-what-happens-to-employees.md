## The question nobody wants to ask out loud

Most business owners who are thinking about selling have one question that sits heavier than all the others, but they are often reluctant to say it directly: what is going to happen to my people?

The employees who have been there for ten or fifteen years. The manager who could probably run the place without you. The folks who showed up every day and made the business what it is. You feel a responsibility to them that has nothing to do with the sale itself — and you want to know, before you sign anything, that you are not handing them over to someone who is going to dismantle what you built.

This is a legitimate concern, and it deserves a straight answer.

## What a buyer actually wants from your team

Most buyers — and this is especially true of operators buying businesses to run and hold, as opposed to financial firms focused on short-term returns — do not want to walk in the door and start making cuts. The team is part of what makes the business worth buying.

A buyer who acquires your company and then loses the key people who made it run has a serious problem on their hands. They paid for a business with certain capabilities, and now those capabilities are walking out the door. That outcome is bad for everyone.

The more sophisticated the buyer, the more they understand this. A buyer who has been through an acquisition before knows that the biggest single risk in a transition is losing the people who keep operations running while the new owner is still learning the business. Their incentive is to retain the team, not disrupt it.

That said, there are things that are genuinely outside anyone's control.

## What no buyer can promise

No buyer who is being honest with you can promise that every job will exist forever, in its current form, without change. Business conditions change. Markets shift. Decisions get made over the course of ownership that nobody can predict at the moment of closing.

Any buyer who tells you nothing will ever change is either not being honest or has not thought carefully about what that commitment means over a ten-year horizon. Some things will change. That is not a reason not to sell — it is just the reality of any ownership transition, including a family transition.

What a buyer can honestly say is what their intentions are, what their track record looks like, and how they think about the people inside a business. Those are things you should ask about directly during the process, and the answers should be part of how you evaluate who you sell to.

## The difference buyer intent makes

The type of buyer matters enormously here, and it is worth understanding before you start talking to anyone.

A financial buyer — a private equity firm or someone structuring the business for a future resale — is making decisions with an exit in mind. Headcount and compensation are expenses that affect the financial performance they will eventually sell on. That does not mean they always cut aggressively, but their decision-making framework is different from someone who intends to own the business for the long term.

A buyer who is acquiring the business to operate it permanently has a different relationship with the team. The people are not a line item to be managed for a future sale — they are the people the buyer is going to be working alongside for years. The incentives around treating them well are much more durable.

## How to protect your people during the process

The most concrete thing you can do as a seller is to be direct about your team during negotiations. Ask the buyer to walk you through how they have handled employees in prior acquisitions. Ask what their intentions are for key roles. If there are employees you feel strongly about — people who have been with you for a long time and whose livelihoods feel like your responsibility — say so.

Some sellers negotiate specific employment protections as part of a deal: minimum retention periods, continuation of benefits at comparable levels, commitments around specific roles. These provisions are negotiable, and a buyer who is serious about keeping the team together will not resist them.

You can also be intentional about who you sell to. The buyer's track record and stated values around how they treat the people inside acquired businesses are knowable things. Do the work to understand them before you commit.

## What most employees actually experience

The honest version of most acquisitions, when the buyer is a serious long-term operator: the team stays, the day-to-day does not change dramatically in the early months, and the transition is less dramatic than everyone feared. The uncertainty before the close is usually worse than the reality after it.

The employees who have the hardest time in transitions are typically the ones who find out late, through the rumor mill, without any context about what is happening or why. Clear, honest communication — on a timeline that makes sense for the business — tends to make the transition go better for everyone, including your team.

That conversation, and how to handle it, is worth thinking through carefully before you start a process.
